## 1. The Brand

### Who we are (one paragraph)

**Memo is the only iOS app that combines brain-training games with a screen-time blocker.** Distracting apps stay locked until you complete a brain-training session — you literally have to train your brain to unlock the apps that drain it. Single Pro tier ($6.99/mo, $39.99/yr, 3-day annual trial). Mascot is **Memo**, an irreverent purple brain who patrols your phone like a bouncer.

The app is being renamed from "Memori" → **Memo - Doomscroll Blocker** with v2.0. Subtitle: *Block Apps. Train Your Brain.*

### The USP (the line we lead with)

**The only screen-time blocker that actually trains your brain.**

There are screen-time blockers (Brick, Opal, Forest, one sec) and there are brain trainers (Lumosity, Elevate, Peak, Impulse). Memo is the only app that does both, where playing brain games is what unlocks your blocked apps.

This is TikTok-shaped: *"this app won't let me open Instagram unless I train my brain"* is a viral hook neither category alone can produce.

### Who we're for

- **Gen Z and younger millennials** whose attention and memory feel fried from social media
- People who've tried other blockers (Opal, Brick) and bounced because willpower wasn't enough
- People who've tried other trainers (Lumosity, Elevate) and didn't see the point because there was no consequence to skipping
- People who already deleted TikTok or Instagram once. And re-downloaded. Three times. This year.

### Who we're NOT

- **NOT Lumosity / Elevate** — clinical, science-clad, parental, older. We're competitive sport, not cognitive therapy.
- **NOT Brick / Opal** — minimalist productivity wellness. We're confrontational and gen-z, not zen.
- **NOT Calm / Headspace** — meditation/wellness. We don't sell calm. We sell fighting back.
- **NOT Patagonia / TOMS** — corporate-philanthropy-as-marketing. We give 10% of profit to charity but our pitch isn't "we're good people." It's "the algorithm is winning. Here's the equalizer."

### Founder origin (use this everywhere)

Dylan built Memo because TikTok was rotting his brain. That's the story. It belongs at the top of the App Store description, in TikTok bios, in paywall copy, in interview pitches. Lead with **"I built this because…"**, never with **"Studies show…"**.

This is the most credible voice the brand has — way more authentic than any growth-hacked launch. Lean on it hard.

## 2. The Three Pillars

Three brand pillars, each embodied by specific product features. If a feature doesn't reinforce a pillar, it's weakening the brand.

### Pillar 1 — Resistance

Fighting back against the attention economy. The thing that makes us *not* a wellness app.

**Embodied by:**
- Focus Mode (block apps until you train)
- The 10% charity commitment to Center for Humane Technology
- The brand voice (anti-doomscroll posture, "outgunned" framing, no wellness-speak)
- The flat-subscription / no-ads / no-data-sale model

**Voice flag:** *"Built to fight what's eating your attention."*

### Pillar 2 — Competition

Making brain training a *sport*, not a *chore*. The thing that separates us from Lumosity-clinical and Calm-meditative.

**Embodied by:**
- **Leaderboards** — daily, weekly, and monthly (just added v2.0). The single feature most other competitors don't lean on.
- Brain Score (0–1000 personalized score, the shareable artifact)
- Brain Age (the comparable, screenshot-friendly metric)
- Per-game personal bests + Game Center achievements
- Streaks (visible commitment metric)
- Quarterly community charity vote (we even gamify our giving)

**Voice flag:** *"The algorithm runs leaderboards on your attention. Memo runs leaderboards on your brain."*

**Why this pillar matters strategically:**
- It's the social proof loop the rest of the brand needs. People share competition; nobody shares meditation.
- It's the differentiator from every other app in either category. Brick + Opal aren't competitive. Lumosity + Elevate are clinical-competitive (against your past self), not socially-competitive (against other people).
- Leaderboards are TikTok-shaped — *"#1 player this week trained X minutes"* writes itself as content.
- The competitive loop is the same psychological mechanism social media exploits (variable rewards, social comparison, ranking) but pointed at *training your brain* instead of *killing your time*. We're using the algorithm's tools against it.

**Why it's currently underutilized:**
- Leaderboards are a buried Compete tab — not part of the onboarding pitch
- Paywall doesn't list leaderboards as a feature (it lists 4 features; leaderboards aren't one)
- Leaderboard copy is neutral ("Top players this week") instead of in-character ("This week's brain bouncers" / "Memo's most patrolled" / "Toughest brains this week")
- TikTok content rarely features rankings even though they're ready-made share moments

This is a brand-priority fix, not a v2.0 launch blocker. But every product decision going forward should ask: *does this reinforce the Competition pillar?* If yes, lean in. If no, ask why.

### Pillar 3 — Care

Memo as a character — irreverent, sharp, but on your side. The warmth that prevents the brand from reading as just-angry.

**Embodied by:**
- Memo (the mascot) — Rive moods (happy/neutral/sad), shield-bouncer personification, in-character copy ("MEMO ON PATROL", "bribe memo")
- Memo mission poses — static hero poses for onboarding beats such as lookout, patrol, defiance, unlock, and commitment
- The founder origin story (Dylan was in your shoes)
- The "you're not weak, you're outgunned" reframe (removes user shame)
- Decay warnings, streak-risk notifications, "we got u" empathy beats
- Shield messages with personality ("Caught in 4K", "Bro is COOKED")

**Voice flag:** *"Memo's seen worse. Let's fix this."*

**Why this matters:**
Anger sells the click. Care sells retention. Without the Care pillar, Memo reads as a manifesto instead of a tool you actually use. Every fear-stat needs to exit with hope; every block needs to feel like Memo's looking out for you, not punishing you.

Mission poses are allowed when the standard mascot set cannot carry the moment. They must stay recognizably Memo: blue brain, purple outline, pink folds, glasses, clean rounded silhouette, and no accidental extra limbs.

### How the pillars stack

In any single piece of copy, ideally **at least two pillars show up**. Three is the gold standard. Examples:

- *"You're not weak. You're outgunned. Memo's the equalizer."* → **Resistance** + **Care**
- *"This week's brain bouncers."* (leaderboard header) → **Competition** + **Care**
- *"Built to fight brain rot. Backed by the people exposing it."* → **Resistance** + **Care** (charity)
- *"Beat the algorithm. Beat your friends. Beat yesterday's score."* → all three.

If a piece of copy hits zero pillars, it's not on-brand. Rewrite it.

## 3. The Enemy

We don't punch at users. We punch at a system. **Two fronts**, both documented public fact, neither conspiracy.

### Front 1: Engagement engineering

Social media giants spend billions designing apps to keep you scrolling — A/B testing every swipe, notification, feed-rank tweak to maximize the time you stay inside their walls. This is not theory; it's the entire business model.

**Defensible facts:**
- ~$57B/year spent on engagement engineering across major platforms (rough industry figure)
- TikTok runs ~50,000 A/B experiments per year
- Average TikTok user: 95 minutes/day
- Average smartphone user: 80+ notifications/day, 4 hours of screen time
- 47 apps installed on the average phone, all fighting for attention

The hook line: *"You're not weak. You're outgunned."*

### Front 2: Data colonization (surveillance capitalism)

Social media giants don't just steal your time — they extract your behavior data and sell it to advertisers. Apple's App Tracking Transparency prompt is the public admission. **85% of users opted out** when given the choice. The other 15% are still tracked.

**Defensible facts:**
- $237B Google ad revenue (2023)
- $134B Meta ad revenue (2023)
- $700B+ global ad-tech industry
- 1.7 MB/second of data generated per online user
- 3,000+ ads/day seen by the average US adult, mostly behavior-targeted
- ~$100–$327/year per US user in extracted ad value

The hook line: *"You're not the customer. You're the inventory."*

### Combined framing

When one line has to capture both fronts:
- **"Free apps take your time AND your data. Memo blocks them both."**
- **"They engineered the algorithm. They built the ad system. You're working two jobs you didn't apply for."**
- **"The attention economy is two industries: stealing your time, and selling what you do with it. Memo's the off switch for both."**

### Why this framing works

- **Universally recognized.** Everyone has seen the ATT prompt. Everyone has felt the algorithm. We don't need to educate users — they already feel both fronts viscerally.
- **Apple-friendly.** Apple themselves has been hammering surveillance capitalism since 2021 with ATT. We're saying what Apple says — App Review will not flag this.
- **Real product differentiator.** Memo has no ads, no data sale, charges a flat subscription. We can credibly punch at ad-supported apps because we're not one.
- **Not partisan.** This is anti-attention-economy and anti-surveillance — those concerns cross political lines. Stay out of explicit politics.

### How we name the enemy

Product UI may name specific consumer apps when the line gets sharper: **TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Snapchat, X, Reddit**. This works best when the copy is about the user's lived behavior: opening TikTok before bed, getting dragged into Instagram Reels, or earning back the apps they actually use.

Still avoid sounding like a legal complaint. **Punch the behavior first, the company second.** The enemy is doomscrolling, dopamine farming, algorithm rot, and ad-supported attention extraction. Named apps are proof points, not the whole personality.

Use category-level language when the claim is broad or strategic: *"social media giants," "the algorithm," "the system," "free apps."* Use specific app names when concreteness makes the moment more screenshot-worthy.

Avoid corporate/legal parent-company names in product UI unless the stat specifically requires them. *"TikTok"* lands. *"ByteDance"* sounds like a deposition.

## 4. The Voice

### Tone reference

**Duolingo × NYT Games × Brick.** Playful + literate + sharp. Not Lumosity-clinical. Not Calm-wellness. Not Apple-corporate.

The vibe is: *Gen Z + slightly pissed off + funny + competitive + caring about you specifically.*

### The 3 voice rules

1. **Pair outrage with agency, always.** Every "they're stealing your attention" line gets a "here's what you do instead" line. Outrage gets the click; agency converts. Brick / Opal / Light Phone all win on agency, not outrage.
2. **Founder story = origin story.** Dylan built this because TikTok was rotting his brain. Most credible voice the brand has. Lead with *"I built this because…"*, not *"Studies show…"*.
3. **Never preachy, never wellness-speak.** No "digital wellbeing," "mindfulness," "intentional tech use." We play resistance + sport, not wellness.

### The "informed fear + defiance" mode (onboarding & marketing)

The strongest emotional lever is **informed fear stacked with defiance**. Not vague ("doomscrolling is bad") but specific ("$57B was spent engineering this. Are you really going to let them win?").

Two levers, always stacked:
- **Informed fear** — verifiable industry-level facts that make the user mad, not anxious
- **Defiance / pride** — refusal to be the loser, refusal to be the product

The unlock line: **"You're not weak. You're outgunned."** Removes self-blame, reframes as adversary problem, sets up the app as the equalizer.

Every fear-stat pairs with a defiance line. Always exit with hope.

Full line bank: see `~/.claude/.../memory/project_brand_voice.md`.

### Do say / don't say

| Do say | Don't say |
|---|---|
| TikTok, Instagram, YouTube, Snap, social media giants | ByteDance legalese, naming companies without a sharp user-facing reason |
| Brain rot, doomscrolling, dopamine farming | Toxic apps, addictive platforms |
| Take your brain back | Reclaim digital wellbeing |
| Block the noise | Reduce screen time |
| Earn your screen time | Reward yourself for productivity |
| Built to fight | Designed to support |
| You're not the customer, you're the inventory | Privacy-first |
| The algorithm is winning | Distractions are everywhere |
| Outgunned | Addicted |
| Stop being the product | Be more mindful |

### Tagline bank

- **Train your brain. Block the noise.** *(currently in the codebase — make it the everywhere line)*
- Take your brain back.
- Built to fight brain rot.
- The app social media giants don't want you to download.
- Your attention isn't free real estate.
- Stop being the product. Start training the brain.
- Brain training that locks the apps making you dumber.
- Doomscrolling is a choice. So is this.

### What NEVER to do

- Make the app a manifesto — it's a useful tool with a sharp opinion, not a protest
- Turn every screen into a named-company rant — specific app names are seasoning, not the meal
- Make every line angry — anger is a hook, not a personality. Funny + sharp + caring beats angry + preachy
- Lose the competitive sport energy (leaderboards, ranks, Brain Score) — those are the carrot to the resistance stick
- Use "addiction" or "addicted" without immediately reframing — lands as user-blame
- Cite specific research papers / academic studies — academic chrome kills voice
- Use "they" without antecedent — sounds paranoid
- Drift political — anti-attention-economy and anti-surveillance, NOT anti-tech-or-capitalism

### Tone-check question

When in doubt, ask: *"Would a 22-year-old who just deleted TikTok for the third time this month screenshot this and send it to a friend?"* If yes, ship it. If it sounds like a wellness app — or worse, a doctor's office — rewrite it.

## 6. The Channels

### Primary — TikTok (@dylanjaws)

The personal indie-dev account, NOT a separate brand handle. The founder IS the brand on TikTok. Posting cadence: **1–2x/day**, replying to every comment.

**Content types in active rotation:**

1. **Founder origin / journey videos** — "Day [N] of building an app to fight social media giants." Use this for milestone moments, real talk about the build, struggles. *Authenticity beat — humanizes the brand.*
2. **AI slideshow content** — 6 prompt sets in the Notion marketing doc (brain age, memory, reaction time, etc.). Algorithm-friendly format, good for cold audiences.
3. **Brain age reveals** — users share their score. "My brain age was 43 and I'm 21." *Viral candy — most shareable format.*
4. **Anti-algorithm hot takes** — using the brand voice + defensible stats. *"Did you know TikTok runs 50,000 A/B tests per year on you?"* Educational fear that sells.
5. **App tour / mechanic explainers** — "This is how Memo works. The app blocker that requires brain training to unlock." *Conversion drivers — show the unique wedge.*
6. **Quarterly charity content** — 4-beat cadence (see below). *Credibility + recurring engagement.*

**No link in bio until 1K followers.** Use *"search Memo on the App Store"* until then. After 1K, link to the App Store + /giving page.

### Secondary — Instagram Reels (cross-post from TikTok)

Repost top-performing TikToks. Don't optimize separately. Different audience overlap, easy reach.

### Tertiary — App Store ASO

The brain-training keyword space is locked by Lumosity / Elevate / Peak (each 100K+ reviews). We will not rank there. **Win on the v2.0 keyword cluster instead:** doomscroll, unscroll, screentime, block, dopamine, attention. The Northstar agent verified `doomscroll` is winnable (diff 37 / pop 39).

ASO is supporting infrastructure, not the growth engine. TikTok is the growth engine.

### 30-day challenge

The marketing strategy is: **"Can I get 10,000 users in 30 days?"** — the founder runs a daily TikTok series documenting the launch attempt. Every video is in this format until day 30. Compounds: each day's video has built-in audience expectation for the next. See Notion marketing doc for details.

### UGC campaign

$500 budget UGC creator campaign — brain age reactions + challenge creators. Started 2026-03-26. Runs in parallel with the founder-led content.

### Quarterly charity content cadence (4 beats)

This is the sustained recurring engagement loop after launch:

- **Week 1 — Shortlist drop:** new TikTok video, "Q[X] is here. Here are the 4 vetted causes. Vote in comments." Opens 8-week vote window.
- **Week 4 — Vote update:** "Halfway through Q[X]. Current leader: [X]. Running pool: $[Y]." Re-engages audience.
- **Week 8 — Winner announce:** "You picked [X]. Donation drops in 30 days." Creates anticipation.
- **Week 12 — Receipt drop:** "Q[X] receipt. We donated $[A] to CHT and $[B] to [winner]." Posts actual receipts.

= **16 high-quality content beats per year** without the math/admin overhead of monthly donations. Templates in `docs/marketing/charity_assets.md`.

## 8. The Promises

These are the public commitments. Everything we ship has to honor them.

1. **No ads, ever.** Memo is subscription-only. The day we add an ad is the day we become what we punched at.
2. **No data sold, ever.** Brain training data stays on device or in iCloud. PostHog analytics are anonymous behavioral events with no PII.
3. **10% of profit donated quarterly.** 5% to Center for Humane Technology, 5% to a community-voted cause. Public ledger with receipts.
4. **No lifetime tier.** Subscription forces us to keep earning your money.
5. **Founder publishes the build journey.** Not because it's a marketing tactic — because accountability is the brand.

Break a promise = break the brand. Don't break a promise.
